1. Linguistic Style
(1) Rational–Official
Formal, restrained, objective, and neutral in tone; resembles institutional statements, official documents, or professional reports; minimal emotional expression.

(2) Colloquial–Emotional
Informal, conversational, and expressive; contains subjective feelings, attitudes, or emotional coloring, often using everyday or vivid language.

2. Expression Type
(1) Fact
Presents objective, verifiable information or explanatory context without expressing subjective attitudes or evaluative judgments.

(2) Opinion
Based on the known public evidence, clearly and strongly expresses subjective attitudes, value judgments, personal interpretations, or evaluative viewpoints.

(3) Corrective
Based on the known public evidence, from the speaker's own understanding, refutes or corrects facts or opinions that are inconsistent with the speaker's own knowledge.

3. Message Granularity
(1) Macro / High-Level
Focuses on overall trends, broader context, or general descriptions without detailed specifics.

(2) Micro / Detailed
Includes concrete cases, examples, numbers, individuals, or fine-grained information.

4. Causal Structure
(1) Direct Causation
States that A directly causes B, with a clear and immediate causal link.

(2) Indirect Causation
States that A influences B through intermediate factors or multi-step causal chains.

(3) Causal Refutation
Rejects, disputes, or corrects a proposed causal relationship, asserting that “A does not / cannot / did not cause B.”

(4) No Causation
Contains no causal relationship and does not address or refute causality.

5. Stance Polarity
(1) Support
Clearly expresses agreement with a viewpoint, policy, action, person, or conclusion.

(2) Oppose
Clearly expresses disagreement, criticism, or objection.

(3) Neutral
Expresses no stance; presents information or viewpoints without favoring any side.

(4) Multi-perspective Comparison
Presents and contrasts multiple positions or viewpoints without endorsing any single one.

6. Veracity Level
(1) TRUE – The statement is accurate and there’s nothing significant missing.

(2) MOSTLY TRUE – The statement is accurate but needs clarification or additional information.

(3) HALF TRUE – The statement is partially accurate but leaves out important details or takes things out of context.

(4) MOSTLY FALSE – The statement contains an element of truth but ignores critical facts that would give a different impression.

(5) FALSE – The statement is not accurate.

(6) PANTS ON FIRE – The statement is not accurate and makes a ridiculous claim.
